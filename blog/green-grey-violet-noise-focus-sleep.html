Green, Grey, and Violet Noise: Definitions and Listening Guide

July 9, 2026 · Sleep · Focus · Sounds · Sensory

Green noise, grey noise, and violet noise are labels for different spectral shapes, but the labels do not carry equal precision. Violet noise has a conventional technical meaning. Grey noise is defined relative to a hearing model. “Green noise” is especially inconsistent across apps, videos, and articles. Two tracks with the same label may not sound alike.

None of these three colors is established as a treatment for ADHD, anxiety, insomnia, tinnitus, or sensory processing differences. Most health-related noise research concerns white or pink noise, not green, grey, or violet. Use these terms to compare sound profiles—not to infer a clinical effect.

Green noise: the least standardized label

“Green noise” has no single, broadly accepted engineering definition comparable with white, pink, or violet noise. It is often marketed as emphasizing a middle-frequency region, resembling a nature-like wash, or using a filtered noise curve with reduced extremes. Those descriptions are not interchangeable.

When evaluating a green-noise recording, ignore claims based on the name and listen to the actual file. Does it have a narrow midrange, audible water or wind, strong bass, or high-frequency hiss? Is it synthetic noise or a nature recording? The answers are more useful than the color label. Do not assume a “500 Hz” marketing description means the track contains only, peaks exactly at, or has a validated effect at that frequency.

Grey noise: shaped around perceived loudness

Grey noise is generally shaped so that a listener perceives a more even loudness across frequencies. Because human hearing sensitivity varies by frequency and level, creating that perceptual balance requires a weighting model. Different equal-loudness assumptions, playback levels, speakers, and individual hearing can produce different results.

A grey-noise track may therefore sound less obviously bright than white noise, but “perceptually equal” is not universal. Age, hearing history, device speakers, and room acoustics all affect the experience. Grey noise is a technical listening option, not a prescription for concentration or sleep.

Violet noise: strong high-frequency emphasis

Violet noise—also called purple noise—rises in power as frequency increases, commonly described as increasing by about 6 decibels per octave. It is the high-frequency counterpart to noise profiles that tilt downward. On ordinary speakers it can sound thin, sharp, or hiss-like because much of its energy is in the upper range.

That brightness can become uncomfortable quickly, especially through headphones or at high volume. Do not raise the level simply because small speakers reproduce low frequencies poorly or because the track seems quieter than a bass-heavy option. If it feels piercing, fatiguing, or aggravates tinnitus, stop.

What research does—and does not—support

A meta-analysis found small overall effects of white or pink noise on task performance in children and young adults with ADHD or elevated ADHD symptoms, alongside worse performance in non-ADHD comparison groups (white/pink noise ADHD meta-analysis). A later clinical study also examined white and pink noise, not these three colors (clinical ADHD noise study).

Those findings cannot be transferred automatically to green, grey, or violet noise, adults in general, anxiety treatment, or sleep. Similarly, a broad review of auditory stimulation for sleep found heterogeneous methods and results (sleep-audio review). Preference may be real and useful to an individual, but it is not proof of a general therapeutic effect.

A neutral three-track listening test

  1. Use the same device, output, room, and task for all three tracks.
  2. Match them at a comfortable perceived level; do not rely only on the slider position.
  3. Listen to green, grey, and violet for five minutes each, with a quiet break between tracks.
  4. Record only observable outcomes: distraction, irritation, masking, urge to change volume, and task completion.
  5. Repeat once on another day before choosing one—or deciding silence is better.

Avoid testing at bedtime for the first time. A daytime audition makes it easier to stop a bright or irritating sound without disrupting a sleep routine. For the broader color system, see the colored-noise guide.

Safe volume and practical playback

WHO recommends keeping personal audio below 60% of maximum as a general precaution, reducing listening time as level rises, and taking listening breaks (WHO safe listening). Because devices and tracks vary, start much lower and increase only if necessary.

Prefer speakers when practical, avoid sleeping in earbuds, and use a timer when continuous playback adds no value. The iPhone Lock Screen setup guide explains background playback. For bedtime context, use the sensory wind-down routine.
