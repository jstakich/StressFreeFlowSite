Brown Noise vs. Pink Noise: An Acoustic Comparison

July 11, 2026 · Sleep · Focus · Sounds

Brown noise and pink noise are both random-noise signals with more low-frequency energy than white noise. They differ in how quickly energy falls as frequency rises. That acoustic difference changes their character, but it does not make either one a treatment for ADHD, anxiety, insomnia, or another condition.

The core difference: spectral slope

Pink noise has equal energy per octave, commonly summarized as power decreasing by about 3 decibels per octave as frequency increases. Because octaves contain more individual frequencies at the high end, this slope prevents the treble-heavy impression of white noise. Listeners often describe pink noise as broad, balanced, or rain-like.

Brown noise—also called Brownian or red noise—falls more steeply, commonly around 6 decibels per octave. It therefore concentrates more energy in lower frequencies and may sound like a deep rush or rumble. “Brown” refers to Brownian motion, not the color brown.

  • Pink: gentler high frequencies, but still a broad audible spectrum.
  • Brown: stronger low-frequency emphasis and less apparent hiss.
  • Neither: inherently louder, safer, more calming, or more effective.

Playback equipment can outweigh the label

Phone speakers reproduce deep bass poorly, so brown noise may sound thin or quiet on one device and heavy on a larger speaker. Headphones can make low frequencies more apparent. Compression, equalization, loop length, and added nature sounds also change a recording. Compare actual tracks through the output you will use rather than comparing names.

Volume matching matters. If one track is louder, listeners may mistake loudness for stronger masking or effectiveness. Set each to a similarly comfortable perceived level, then lower it until it sits behind the task.

Also inspect whether a track is truly continuous noise or a processed soundscape. A recording labeled “brown noise” might include a low-pass filter, boosted bass, stereo movement, or a short loop; a “pink noise” track might include rain or softened transients. Those production choices can be more noticeable than the textbook spectral slope. If a loop clicks, pulses, or changes level, reject that file rather than concluding that the entire noise color is unsuitable. For a fair comparison, use plain tracks from the same source when possible and disable spatial-audio or equalizer settings that alter one playback session but not the other.

For environmental masking

Masking works when one sound makes another less noticeable. Pink noise’s broader high-frequency content may cover some speech or sharper household sounds more readily; brown noise may make low rumbles less salient. Room acoustics and the unwanted sound’s frequency content determine the result. Increasing volume is not always the answer.

First reduce the source when possible: close a door, move away from an appliance, or use soft furnishings. Then add the quietest background level that helps. If neither signal masks the target without becoming distracting, use another sound or silence.

What ADHD research actually studied

A meta-analysis reported small overall improvements in task performance from white or pink noise among children and young adults with ADHD or elevated ADHD symptoms, but worse performance in non-ADHD comparison groups (ADHD noise meta-analysis). The result does not establish an individual prescription, and it does not support brown noise as an ADHD treatment.

A clinical study likewise evaluated white and pink noise in children with ADHD (clinical ADHD noise study). Neither study shows that pink noise treats ADHD, that brown noise reduces anxiety, or that either replaces evidence-based care. Adults and real-world work settings may not respond like study participants.

What sleep research can tell us

Auditory sleep studies vary in participants, sound type, timing, and outcome measures. A systematic review described heterogeneous evidence rather than a universal benefit (auditory stimulation sleep review). It is reasonable to test whether a steady sound makes your room more predictable, but neither brown nor pink noise guarantees faster sleep or fewer awakenings.

Put sound inside a broader routine. NHLBI recommends consistent timing, quiet pre-bed activity, and a cool, dark, quiet room (NHLBI sleep habits). The sensory bedtime guide turns those principles into a low-pressure sequence.

A fair brown-versus-pink test

  1. Pick one task—reading, routine desk work, or settling at bedtime.
  2. Use the same speaker, position, session length, and room conditions.
  3. Match comfortable perceived volume, then listen to pink noise for 10–15 minutes.
  4. On a separate comparable session, test brown noise for the same duration.
  5. Record distraction, comfort, masking, task completion, and any after-effects.
  6. Repeat before deciding; keep neither if silence performs better.

Do not use a single unusually good or difficult day as proof. Also avoid testing while driving, supervising hazards, or doing work where reduced awareness of alarms and people would be unsafe.

Interpret the notes narrowly. If pink noise covered nearby conversation more effectively, that result applies to that room, speaker position, volume, and type of conversation. If brown noise felt less intrusive during reading, it does not follow that it will improve demanding analysis or sleep. A useful outcome can be conditional: one track for a noisy afternoon, the other for low-level ventilation hum, and neither in a quiet bedroom. Recheck occasionally if equipment or surroundings change, but avoid turning everyday listening into constant optimization. The practical winner is simply the option that reduces a specific distraction without introducing a new one.

Listen safely

WHO recommends keeping personal-device volume below 60% of maximum as a general safe-listening measure and limiting duration as sound level rises (WHO safe listening). Start substantially lower, take breaks, and prefer speakers when practical. Stop for pain, muffled hearing, ringing, headache, or sensory distress.
