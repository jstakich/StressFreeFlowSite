Brown Noise vs. Pink Noise: An Acoustic Comparison

July 11, 2026 · Sleep · Focus · Sounds

Brown noise and pink noise are both random-noise signals with more low-frequency energy than white noise. Pink tends to sound broad and rain-like; brown is deeper, softer at the top end, and often described as a steady rush. Either can be a useful background layer when it makes conversation, traffic, appliances, or other intermittent sounds less noticeable. The better choice is usually the one that feels comfortable at low volume and fits the sound you want to mask.

Pink noise has direct, though still developing, research relevant to task performance in people with ADHD or elevated ADHD symptoms and to sleep in noisy environments. Brown noise has a different practical appeal: its deep low-frequency character is preferred by some listeners for desk routines, sensory comfort, or winding down. This comparison keeps those strengths separate so you can choose by sound, context, and personal response.

Pink noise: balanced coverage with research support

Pink noise has equal energy per octave, commonly summarized as power decreasing by about 3 decibels per octave as frequency increases. Because octaves contain more individual frequencies at the high end, this slope prevents the treble-heavy impression of white noise. Listeners often describe pink noise as broad, balanced, or rain-like.

That broad spread can be practical when the competing sound includes voices, footsteps, doors, or traffic rather than only a low mechanical hum. Pink noise can sit behind reading, paperwork, or a repeatable focus block, creating a more consistent acoustic backdrop. At bedtime, the same continuity may help soften changes in an otherwise noisy room. Keep it quiet enough to fade into the background instead of becoming the main event.

A 2024 meta-analysis found small, statistically significant task-performance benefits from white or pink noise in children and young adults with ADHD or elevated ADHD symptoms (ADHD white/pink noise meta-analysis). The studies differed in participants, tasks, and sound delivery, so the most practical takeaway is to treat pink noise as a reasonable option to test during a defined task, while tracking whether it helps you begin, persist, or recover from distraction.

Brown noise: deep, smooth, and easy to personalize

Brown noise—also called Brownian or red noise—falls more steeply, commonly around 6 decibels per octave. It therefore concentrates more energy in lower frequencies and may sound like a deep rush or rumble. “Brown” refers to Brownian motion, not the color brown.

For listeners who find brighter hiss distracting, brown noise’s reduced high-frequency presence can feel smoother and less busy. It is well suited to preference-led experimentation: try it behind routine computer work, journaling, stretching, or a quiet pre-sleep activity. Its low-end emphasis may also blend comfortably with ventilation, distant road rumble, or bass transmitted through a building.

Direct clinical research on brown noise is limited, so its case rests on acoustic fit rather than a health claim. That is still useful information. A sound does not need to be a clinical intervention to serve as a predictable cue for starting a focus session, covering an irritating environmental texture, or marking the transition into a wind-down routine. Comfort and repeatability are worthwhile criteria.

  • Choose pink for broader masking, a rain-like balance, or the profile with direct ADHD-task and emerging noisy-sleep research.
  • Choose brown for a deeper sound, less apparent hiss, or a low-frequency background that feels easier to ignore.
  • Keep both available if one fits daytime concentration and the other fits evening wind-down.

Matching each profile to the environment

Masking works when a steady sound reduces the contrast between the background and a distracting event. Pink noise’s wider frequency coverage may blend more readily with speech and sharper household sounds. Brown noise can be a comfortable match for lower rumbles. The target sound matters: a neighbor’s voice, an air conditioner, and occasional traffic each occupy different parts of the spectrum.

At home, begin by reducing the source when possible—close a door, increase distance, or soften reflections—then add the quietest pink or brown layer that makes the remaining sound easier to overlook. Increasing playback volume is rarely the best first move. Repositioning a speaker closer to you, but away from the wall or unwanted sound, may improve coverage while keeping the overall listening level comfortable.

Playback equipment can change the experience

Phone speakers reproduce deep bass poorly, so brown noise may sound thin or quiet on one device and heavy on a larger speaker. Headphones can make low frequencies more apparent. Compression, equalization, loop length, and added nature sounds also change a recording. Compare actual tracks through the output you will use rather than comparing names.

Volume matching matters. If one track is louder, listeners may mistake loudness for stronger masking or effectiveness. Set each to a similarly comfortable perceived level, then lower it until it sits behind the task.

Also inspect whether a track is truly continuous noise or a processed soundscape. A recording labeled “brown noise” might include a low-pass filter, boosted bass, stereo movement, or a short loop; a “pink noise” track might include rain or softened transients. Those production choices can be more noticeable than the textbook spectral slope. If a loop clicks, pulses, or changes level, reject that file rather than concluding that the entire noise color is unsuitable. For a fair comparison, use plain tracks from the same source when possible and disable spatial-audio or equalizer settings that alter one playback session but not the other.

Pink noise and noisy sleep environments

Sleep-related evidence is most relevant when sound is used to soften an intrusive environment. In a crossover pilot involving 12 adults, pink noise attenuated some traffic-noise-related sleep and metabolic disruptions (pink-noise traffic crossover pilot). Its small sample makes it emerging evidence, but the design directly supports a practical question: whether steady pink noise can reduce the impact of variable nighttime traffic.

A separate study of 10 adults living in a noisy New York City environment found that broadband white noise improved sleep latency and wake-after-sleep-onset (white-noise study in a high-noise environment). Although white noise is acoustically different, the result reinforces the broader masking rationale: steady sound can be especially relevant where unpredictable environmental noise is the problem. For a quiet bedroom, preference and comfort may matter more than adding any sound.

Put sound inside a broader routine. NHLBI recommends consistent timing, quiet pre-bed activity, and a cool, dark, quiet room (NHLBI sleep habits). The sensory bedtime guide turns those principles into a low-pressure sequence.

A low-volume brown-versus-pink A/B test

  1. Choose one purpose: masking conversation, supporting a focus block, or winding down.
  2. Use the same speaker, position, session length, and room conditions.
  3. Start very low, match comfortable perceived volume, and try pink for 10–15 minutes.
  4. In a comparable session, test brown for the same duration and task.
  5. Note comfort, masking, task starts, attention shifts, and how often you notice the track.
  6. Repeat on another day, then assign each sound to the context where it fits best.

Look for a modest, repeatable improvement in the listening environment rather than a dramatic feeling. A useful focus profile often becomes less noticeable after a few minutes. A useful bedtime profile should remain comfortable as the room gets quieter. Avoid testing while driving, supervising hazards, or doing work where reduced awareness of alarms and people would be unsafe.

Your notes can be context-specific: pink may cover nearby conversation more effectively, while brown may feel less intrusive during reading or evening routines. Speaker position, volume, and room acoustics can change the result. Recheck after changing equipment or surroundings, but keep the process simple. The practical winner is the option that reduces a specific distraction and remains pleasant enough to use consistently.

Listen safely

WHO recommends keeping personal-device volume below 60% of maximum as a general safe-listening measure and limiting duration as sound level rises (WHO safe listening). Start substantially lower, take breaks, and prefer speakers when practical. Stop for pain, muffled hearing, ringing, headache, or sensory distress.

Sources and scope

This acoustic comparison combines established spectral definitions with research that informs practical listening. Pink noise is represented directly in the ADHD meta-analysis and traffic-noise pilot; the high-noise sleep study used white noise, and the office study examined stationary masking. Brown noise is presented on its documented acoustic character and its value as a preference-led masking option, without assigning it a clinical effect. Together, these sources support careful experimentation with the sound profile, room, task, and volume kept clearly in view.
